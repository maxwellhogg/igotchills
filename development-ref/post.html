Halloween Ends bombs at the box-office... But is it really as bad as reviewers suggest?

posted by Maxwell Hogg

The Halloween franchise has been a staple of horror cinema for over four decades, with Michael Myers becoming an iconic figure in the genre. The latest installment, *Halloween Ends*, was highly anticipated as the conclusion to David Gordon Green’s trilogy, which began with the well-received *Halloween* (2018). However, the film’s release was met with mixed reviews and a disappointing box office performance. But was it really as bad as critics claimed, or does it deserve a second look from fans?

The Box Office Disappointment

*Halloween Ends* opened to a modest $40 million domestically in its first weekend, a significant drop from its predecessor, *Halloween Kills*, which debuted with $49 million in 2021. While $40 million might sound like a solid opening for a horror film, it fell short of expectations for a franchise finale, especially one with such a storied legacy. The film’s global earnings barely crossed $100 million, a far cry from the $255 million haul of the 2018 reboot.

Several factors contributed to this underwhelming performance. The decision to release the film simultaneously in theaters and on Peacock (NBCUniversal’s streaming platform) likely cannibalized its box office potential. Additionally, the mixed reception of *Halloween Kills* may have dampened audience enthusiasm for the trilogy’s conclusion. But does the box office tell the whole story?

Critical Reception: A Divisive Finale

Critics were quick to pan *Halloween Ends*, with many calling it a disappointing end to the trilogy. The film currently holds a 40% rating on Rotten Tomatoes, with critics criticizing its tonal shifts, pacing, and departure from the traditional slasher formula. Some felt that the film’s attempt to explore deeper themes, such as trauma and the cycle of violence, came at the expense of the straightforward thrills that fans expected.

However, not all reviews were negative. Some critics praised the film for taking risks and trying something different. Jamie Lee Curtis’s performance as Laurie Strode was widely applauded, and the film’s bold narrative choices were seen as a refreshing departure from the predictable slasher tropes. The decision to focus on a new character, Corey Cunningham (played by Rohan Campbell), and his descent into darkness was a polarizing but intriguing choice that added layers to the story.

Fan Reactions: Love It or Hate It

Fan reactions to *Halloween Ends* were equally divided. While some viewers appreciated the film’s ambition and emotional depth, others felt it strayed too far from what made the franchise beloved in the first place. The reduced screen time for Michael Myers and the focus on Corey’s story left many fans feeling unsatisfied, especially those expecting a more traditional showdown between Laurie and Michael.

On the other hand, some fans applauded the film for its willingness to take risks and explore new territory. The themes of generational trauma and the idea of evil being a contagious force resonated with certain viewers, who found the film to be a thought-provoking conclusion to Laurie Strode’s arc.

Is It Really That Bad?

The answer to whether *Halloween Ends* is as bad as critics say depends on what you’re looking for in a Halloween movie. If you’re expecting a straightforward slasher with Michael Myers wreaking havoc, you might be disappointed. However, if you’re open to a more ambitious and thematic approach to the franchise, *Halloween Ends* offers a compelling, if flawed, conclusion.

The film’s biggest strength lies in its willingness to take risks and subvert expectations. While not all of these choices land perfectly, they make *Halloween Ends* a unique entry in the franchise. Jamie Lee Curtis delivers a powerful performance, and the film’s exploration of trauma and evil adds depth to the story.

Ultimately, *Halloween Ends* may not be the finale that everyone wanted, but it’s far from the disaster that some critics have made it out to be. It’s a film that dares to be different, and for that, it deserves credit—even if it doesn’t fully stick the landing.

Final Verdict

*Halloween Ends* may have been a box office flop, but it’s not without its merits. While it may not satisfy all fans or critics, it’s a bold and ambitious attempt to bring the franchise to a close. Whether you love it or hate it, one thing is certain: *Halloween Ends* will be remembered as one of the most divisive entries in the series. And in a genre that thrives on pushing boundaries, maybe that’s not such a bad thing after all.
